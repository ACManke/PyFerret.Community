On Mon, Dec 17, 2018 at 5:13 PM William Kessler <william.s.kessler@xxxxxxxx> wrote:
The big problem is defining what is onshore; you need the perpendicular component. That is hard to do for an irregular shape defined by gridboxes.

If you are willing to work by coast segments that can be defined by latitude steps it is fairly straightforward. That is: work up the east coast, then down into the Gulf of Carpenteria, then up the west side of the Gulf, then down the west coast to Cape Leuwin, etc. 
For each latitude step find the vector defining the coast slope. You have the vector of your transport at that point. The vector difference will let you find the perpendicular component. That would be tedious ...

Another way is to define a simpler problem: what are the gridpoints inside Australia?

Then you can do this much more easily with the divergence theorem. The integral of the divergence of the vectors inside Australia must equal the total flux through the boundary.

Similarly, if you want the parallel-to-the-coast vector sum, you can use Green's theorem, which says that the curl of the vectors integrated over the area inside the domain equals the line integral of a vector around the boundary of a domain .

Sorry for rambling ... BK
